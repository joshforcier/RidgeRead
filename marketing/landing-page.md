## FEATURES

### 1. Real Terrain Analysis — Not Colored Overlays on a Topo Map

**Headline:** Every ridge, bench, saddle, and drainage — measured and scored.

We pull a 400-point elevation grid from USGS data for your selected area. From that, we compute actual slope angles, aspect directions, and relief — then run feature detection to find saddles (crossing funnels), benches (bedding flats on steep terrain), finger ridges (satellite bull travel), and drainage bottoms (wallows and water). These are real terrain features at real coordinates, not a colored heat blob.

---

### 2. AI-Powered Points of Interest — Placed on Real Ground

**Headline:** POIs that know the difference between a parking lot and a wallow.

Our AI reads verified OpenStreetMap land cover — every mapped road, trail, stream, meadow, forest stand, and building in your area — combined with the computed elevation and slope data. It places points of interest on actual terrain features: a feeding zone on a real meadow within 200m of timber edge, a bedding area on a real north-facing bench at 9,400 feet with 60-80% canopy.

Every POI comes with tactical advice referencing the real elevation, slope, and aspect at that spot.

And we enforce a customizable buffer from every road and trail — set it from 0.1 to 2 miles. If it's near a road, it's not elk habitat. Period.

---

### 3. Season + Time of Day Intelligence

**Headline:** Elk on October 1st at dawn are not the same elk on November 15th at dusk.

TerrainIQ models three distinct seasons with dramatically different behavior:

**Rut (Sep–Oct):** Breeding overrides energy. Bulls bugle from saddles, bed on north-facing benches at 10-30 degrees with 60-80% canopy, stay within 400m of water. Wallows are critical. Travel peaks at dawn and dusk along ridgelines and saddles.

**Post-Rut (Oct–Nov):** Recovery. Bulls go silent, lose 15-20% body weight, hide in the densest north-facing timber available — 80%+ canopy, 20-40 degree slopes, deadfall. They may not leave timber for days. If you can see 50 yards, it's not thick enough.

**Late Season (Nov–Dec):** Survival. Aspect flips — elk bed on south/southwest-facing slopes (15-25 degrees F warmer). Snow replaces water. Large herds on predictable daily patterns between south-facing feeding meadows and south-facing bedding timber. Most patternable elk of the year.

Toggle the season and time in the sidebar. Every POI, every zone, every weight recalculates instantly.

---

### 4. Behavior Layer Overlays

**Headline:** See the why, not just the where.

Five behavior layers, each driven by real terrain scoring:

- **Feeding** — Mapped meadows and grasslands near timber edges. The 100-400m transition zone where timber thins to open is the #1 encounter zone across all seasons.
- **Water** — Confirmed streams, springs, and drainage confluences. Rut: critical (within 400m). Late season: snow replaces it.
- **Bedding** — Aspect and slope matched to season. North-facing benches in dense timber for rut/post-rut. South-facing wind-sheltered slopes for late season.
- **Wallows** — Drainage bottoms near springs within timber. Flat, boggy, low-slope areas at the heads of small drainages. Rut only.
- **Travel Corridors** — Saddles, ridgelines, finger ridges, and the connective terrain between feeding and bedding. Where elk move, and when.

Each layer shows a weight percentage that shifts with your season and time selection — so you can see at a glance that wallows matter 80% during the rut at dusk but 0% in late season.
